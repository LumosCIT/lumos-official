The Rise of Airbnb

Everyone hits a huge wall at least once in their life. A wall so huge that you just can’t seem to find a way around it. Well, three guys from San Francisco couldn’t take it anymore and decided to build their way to the very top of the wall. From having thousands of dollars in debt to becoming a $62 billion company over just 14 years. The eye-opening story of Brian Chesky, Joe Gebbia, and Nathan Blecharczyk is sure to inspire you.

What is Airbnb?

Airbnb Inc., based in San Francisco, California, is an online marketplace that focuses on short-term homestays and place rentals. The company acts as a broker between the host and the tenants. Be it a single room, your own house, or even an unused caravan. Airbnb lets you rent out anything, given that their set of guidelines and requirements are met. Airbnb is a shortened version of its original name, Airbeds and breakfast, which is literally how they started, with a few Airbeds and a morning meal.

How did it all begin?

Although the idea behind it sounds like a walk in the park, it was a subject of huge controversy at that time. The tale of these three youngsters was one filled with blood, sweat, and tears.

It all dates back to October 2007, in San Francisco. Brian Chesky and Joe Gebbia were two friends who had just moved from New York. Struggling between finding a job and having to pay rent, they were looking for a way to earn some cash. With the upcoming Industrial Designers' Conference in San Francisco, there was a shortage of hotel rooms and accommodations.

The pair saw this as an opportunity to end their misery and cooked up an idea. They bought a few airbeds and rented out their apartment loft to designers looking for a place to stay. They patched together a simple site, "airbedandbreakfast.com". The idea was to offer visitors a place to sleep and breakfast in the morning. They charged $80 for a night and saw three people show up at their doorstep soon after. The duo realized that they had stumbled upon a magnificent idea and decided to take it to the next level.

They invited Joe's former roommate, Nathan Blecharczyk, to join the team as the third co-founder and further developed the site. Despite that, they only got 2 bookings when they relaunched, and the site only had 2 users, one of them being the co-founder Chesky.

The Trough of Sorrows

After upgrading the website even more, the trio decided to launch it again in August 2008, just before the Democratic National Convention in Denver. Over 20,000 people were attending the event, and all the hotels were booked out. The trio thought this would be the perfect opportunity to relaunch, so they did. They managed to get some media attention; however, the response and bookings fell short. This part of the Airbnb startup story was what Airbnb founder Brian Chesky called, The Trough of Sorrows. They were in dire straits, with a mountain of debt crushing them as they clutched onto straws to make a living.

The main reason for the minimal response was that people were sceptical about getting into this new idea as they were concerned about their safety. The first comment on the launch publication on TechCrunch illustrates what people thought of the idea

"If this ever becomes mainstream, the whole thing will come crashing down. The kind of people that *Do* rob, abuse, rape, and murder people will start using these systems, and that will spread legitimate fear, corrupting the whole thing."

"Pray that this remains underground; that's the only way it can survive."

- J. Jeffryes

Since the “bed” part of “bed and breakfast” was not getting favourable results, Chesky thought of focusing on the breakfast side of the equation. Riding on the election fever, Chesky and Gebbia thought of creating presidential-themed breakfast cereals, Obama O's and Cap'n McCains. The founders handcrafted numbered and sold each limited edition cereal box for $40 apiece. Although it was difficult to get it moving initially, the Airbnb founders made a whopping $30,000 from these cereal boxes once it caught on. This was the much-needed breath of air that they needed and it almost got them out of debt. They even got national coverage because of this.

The First Funding

They knew that their success was going to be short-lived since the election fever can't go on forever, so they approached Y Combinator for funding in November 2008. Paul Graham of Y Combinator did not immediately budge to their idea, but through the presidential-themed cereal boxes, the venture capitalist was impressed by the founders' spirit and passion. This gave Airbnb it's first funding of $20,000.

At this time, they were making about $200 a week. Now that they had funding, a proper income, and a better work structure, the founders decided to travel to New York, where the majority of their users and listings were situated. They connected with their users, analyzed the listed properties, and formed a better community with their customers. Through this, they realized that a key factor that could be improved about their service was better photographs of properties and better quality of homestays.

To improve this, they went door by door to every property, to ensure the quality as well as take and upload better pictures. They even guided the hosts to give customers the best homestay experience. By the end of this New York trip, they not only managed to form an amazingly supportive community of users but also doubled their weekly income to $400.

From around the beginning of 2009, Airbnb was on a roll. With a supportive and responsive community and a steady rise in users as well as listed properties, Airbnb was unstoppable.

The Takeaways

Life was not a bed of roses yet; there still were many hassles to tackle and walls to climb, but by this time, the founders were more experienced in this regard. They had a stronger foundation, which was built on the sweat and tears of their past. Regardless of anything that stood in their way, the trio continued to march forward, and they still do so. Their platform has expanded gradually to almost every nook and cranny of the world and now stands as the 243rd most valuable company with a market cap of around $62 Billion, as of November 2022.

Airbnb has been one of the hardest hit platforms by COVID-19. At the peak of the pandemic, new bookings were down by 85 per cent, although app usage has gradually increased as countries started to relax their travel restrictions.

Many Airbnb hosts in major cities switched to long-term rentals as the short-term market collapsed in March as a way to maintain a steady income. To keep hosts on the platform, Airbnb recently launched monthly stays, similar to a normal rental service but without the usual yearly rental agreement.

This attests that every company, every organization, and every startup has its ups and downs. There are points where they could be reeling in a big haul as well as times where they could be struggling to stay steady. No matter the situation, one who can competently handle both and adapt quickly is sure to sustain and make a name for themselves. Is forming a startup easy? No. But if the fire in you is not fast enough to flicker, then it is sure to burn and light up the night sky.

As a popular quote by Jack London states,

Life is not always a matter of holding good cards, but sometimes playing a poor hand well.
